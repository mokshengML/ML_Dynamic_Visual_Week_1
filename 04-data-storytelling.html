Data Storytelling

A chart that doesn't tell a story is just decoration

A finding only matters if someone understands and acts on it. Data storytelling is the craft of guiding a viewer's eye, naming the surprise out loud, and giving them somewhere to go next. Three pieces: annotated charts, a narrative arc, and what you say in the room.

Annotated charts Narrative arc What to say
1

Annotated visualisations — guide the eye, name the point

A plain chart asks: "figure it out yourself". An annotated chart says: "here is what to look at, and here is why it matters." Same data, very different impact. Watch one chart grow from raw bars into a story.

🗺️
Real-life analogy
A blank map and a guided tour both cover the same city. The map drops you in and wishes you luck. The guide says "see that building? That's where the story is." Annotations turn your chart into the guide.
Step 1 of 6
Step 0: just bars. No title, no labels. The viewer sees seven mystery rectangles and must guess what they mean.
💡
Rule of thumb
Every chart you ship to humans should answer three questions before they ask: what is this, what should I notice, and why does it matter? Title, highlight, callout — in that order.
2

EDA narrative — the five-beat story arc

Random charts in random order = a noisy report. A good EDA writeup follows a story: Question → Data → Exploration → Finding → Action. Each stage earns the next one. The viewer never has to ask "why are you showing me this?"

🎬
Real-life analogy
Think of a mystery film. Setup (something is wrong) → investigation (clues) → twist (the unexpected finding) → resolution (what we do now). Skip any beat and the audience checks their phone. EDA reports work the same way.
Step 1 of 6
The full arc: five beats your report should hit, in this order. Click Next to walk through each beat.
💡
Test it on yourself
Before sharing an EDA report, read it as a stranger would. Can you point at one sentence for each beat? If "Action" is missing, you have an analysis, not a story.
3

What to say — when the slide is up and the room is quiet

Your slide is the picture. You are the soundtrack. The same chart can land as a yawn or a "what?!" depending on the first sentence out of your mouth. Five small habits make all the difference.

🎤
Real-life analogy
A radio host doesn't just play a song and leave. They introduce it ("you'll want to hear this one"), play it, then say what it meant. Your slides need the same bookends.
Step 1 of 6
Here's your slide. The chart is on screen, the room is quiet, all eyes on you. Click Next for the five habits, one at a time.
💡
One more thing
Practice the first 10 seconds of every slide out loud. If you can't say it cleanly, the slide isn't ready — and no amount of polish on the chart will save you.
