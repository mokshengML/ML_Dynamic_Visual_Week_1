Feature Engineering

Reshape raw data into signals your model can read

Models can't read calendars, can't remember yesterday, and can't tell that "Mumbai" and "Delhi" are different things. Feature engineering is how we translate raw columns into numbers a model finds useful — one careful transformation at a time.

Lag features Rolling averages Date-time split One-hot encoding
1

Lag features — letting the model peek at yesterday

A model sees rows one at a time. By default, it has no memory. If you want it to learn "today's sales depend on yesterday's sales", you must copy yesterday's value into today's row — that's a lag feature.

📞
Real-life analogy
Imagine asking a friend "how busy is the shop today?" — but they can only see right now. To help them spot a trend, you whisper yesterday's answer in their ear before they look. That whisper is a lag feature: past value, glued onto the present row.
Step 1 of 4
Here is a tiny daily-sales table: 6 days, 6 numbers. The model can't see across rows.
# pandas one-liner df['Sales_lag1'] = df['Sales'].shift(1) # shift(2) → 2 days ago, shift(7) → same day last week, etc.
💡
Why it matters
Almost every time-series model — sales, demand, energy, stock — relies on lag features. They turn "memory" into a column the model can multiply.
2

Rolling averages — smoothing the noise out

Raw daily data is jagged. A rolling average slides a small window across the series and replaces each point with the average of its neighbours. The bumps soften; the trend appears.

🧹
Real-life analogy
Think of running a finger over a wrinkled tablecloth: the wrinkles flatten, the table's real shape emerges. A rolling mean does the same with daily noise — the underlying trend stops hiding behind the wiggles.
Step 1 of 4
A noisy daily-sales series. Every day jumps a bit — hard to read a trend off this.
# window=3 → average of today + previous 2 days df['Sales_ma3'] = df['Sales'].rolling(window=3).mean() # Also: .sum(), .std(), .max() — same trick, different summary
💡
Why it matters
Rolling features capture recent momentum without overreacting to a single weird day. They're the second-most-used trick in any time-series toolkit, right after lag features.
3

Date-time decomposition — unpacking a timestamp

"2025-03-14 09:00" is one column to you, but to a model it's a meaningless string. Split it into year, month, day-of-week, hour — and suddenly the model can learn "Mondays are quiet" or "December spikes".

📅
Real-life analogy
A timestamp is like a sealed envelope. Inside are useful clues: which season, which weekday, which hour of the day. The model can't peek inside on its own — you have to open the envelope and lay each clue on the table as its own column.
Step 1 of 6
A single timestamp column. Useless to a tree-based model as-is — it's just a string of digits.
df['ts'] = pd.to_datetime(df['ts']) df['year'] = df['ts'].dt.year df['month'] = df['ts'].dt.month df['day'] = df['ts'].dt.day df['weekday'] = df['ts'].dt.dayofweek # 0=Mon df['hour'] = df['ts'].dt.hour
💡
Why it matters
Cyclical patterns — weekends, paydays, holiday seasons, evening peaks — only become learnable when you expose them as separate columns. Decomposition is almost free, and almost always worth it.
4

One-hot encoding — turning categories into numbers

A column like city = "Mumbai" is a word, not a number. Most models can't multiply words. One-hot encoding replaces a single categorical column with one yes/no column per category.

🗳️
Real-life analogy
Think of a checkbox form: instead of writing your city in a blank, you tick one box from "□ Mumbai · □ Pune · □ Delhi". For each row, exactly one box is ticked (1) and the rest are blank (0). That's one-hot encoding — and it's exactly what the model wants to see.
Step 1 of 5
A single column with 5 rows: each row holds a city name. The model sees strings and gives up.
# pandas does this in one call df_encoded = pd.get_dummies(df, columns=['city']) # scikit-learn equivalent: OneHotEncoder()
💡
Why it matters
Any column of text categories (city, product, payment-type, channel) usually needs encoding before it enters a model. For very high-cardinality columns (1000+ categories), look at target encoding or embeddings instead — one-hot would explode.
