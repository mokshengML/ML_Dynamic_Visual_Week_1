Exploratory Data Analysis

Listen to your data — before you model it

EDA is the "first conversation" with a fresh dataset. We sketch a few quick pictures, ask: what shape is this? are there surprises? and let the data tell us where to look next. Below, each picture builds itself step-by-step.

Distributions Histograms Boxplots Correlation matrix Pair plots
1

Distributions — the "shape" of your numbers

Take any column of numbers (heights, prices, exam marks) and ask: where do most of the values sit, and how spread out are they? That arrangement is the distribution. Before any maths, just look at the shape.

👥
Real-life analogy
If you lined up 100 random adults shortest to tallest, you'd see a few short people on the left, a crowd of average-height people in the middle, and a few tall people on the right. That bell shape is the distribution of human height.
Step 1 of 4
100 random adults. Each dot is one person's height. Right now they're scattered randomly along the line.
💡
Why care?
Many models assume a tidy bell shape. If your data is skewed (long tail on one side), shaped like a camel (two humps), or has a wall (cut-off), the model will silently lie to you. Always look first.
2

Histograms — counting people in height-buckets

A histogram is the simplest way to see a distribution. Chop the range into equal "bins" (160–165, 165–170, 170–175…), drop each value into its bin, and stack them up. The taller the bar, the more values landed there.

🪣
Real-life analogy
Imagine buckets lined up by height range. Each person walks up to the bucket their height belongs in and drops a ball. After everyone's done, the bar of balls in each bucket = the bar in your histogram.
Step 1 of 5
A pile of 60 height values. They sit at the bottom waiting to be counted.
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt plt.hist(df["height"], bins=8, edgecolor="white") plt.xlabel("Height (cm)") plt.ylabel("Count")
3

Boxplots — the 5-number summary, drawn

A boxplot is a tiny diagram that summarises the whole distribution in 5 numbers: the smallest, the 25% mark, the middle, the 75% mark, and the largest. The dots floating outside are the unusual ones — outliers.

📏
Real-life analogy
Think of a school class lined up by height. The shortest and tallest students mark the edges. The middle kid is the median. The middle-50% of the line (the everyday students) is the box. Anyone freakishly tall or short stands apart — an outlier.
Step 1 of 5
A class of 25 students plotted by their test marks. Each dot is one student.
🎯
Read a boxplot in 3 seconds
Box = middle 50% of your data. Line inside box = the median (the middle student). Whiskers = the normal range. Dots outside = outliers worth investigating.
4

Correlation matrix — which columns move together?

Correlation measures whether two columns rise and fall together. A score of +1 = perfect "together" movement, −1 = perfect "opposite", 0 = no relationship. A correlation matrix is just a heatmap of these scores for every pair of columns.

🌡️
Real-life analogy
Ice-cream sales and temperature rise together — strong positive correlation. Heater sales and temperature move opposite — strong negative correlation. Ice-cream sales and a person's age? Probably no link — near zero.
Step 1 of 5
Two columns: temperature and ice-cream sales. Notice how the dots climb together — when one is high, the other is too.
df.corr() # a square table of correlations import seaborn as sns sns.heatmap(df.corr(), annot=True, cmap="coolwarm")
5

Pair plots — every column vs every other column, at once

A pair plot is a grid of small scatter plots: row i, column j shows column i against column j. The diagonal shows each column's own distribution. One picture, every relationship.

🎭
Real-life analogy
Like a round-robin sports tournament: every team plays every other team. A pair plot is the score-board — at a glance you see who beats whom, who's evenly matched, and who's an outlier.
Step 1 of 4
A flower dataset with 3 measurements per flower: sepal length, petal length, petal width. We will make one plot for every pair.
🔍
What to look for
Tight diagonal lines → strong relationships. Cloud-like shapes → no relationship. Distinct colour clusters → your groups (species, customers, regions) really do differ. A pair plot is your first 30 seconds with new data.
